Phishing & Social Engineering

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize phishing attempts in emails and messages
  • Understand social engineering tactics
  • Know how to verify suspicious communications
  • Apply prevention techniques to stay safe

Understanding Phishing

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers disguise as trustworthy entities to steal sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Common Phishing Channels

  • Email: Fake emails appearing from banks, companies, or colleagues
  • SMS/Text: "Smishing" messages with malicious links
  • Phone Calls: "Vishing" - voice phishing attempts
  • Social Media: Fake profiles or direct messages
  • Fake Websites: Look-alike sites designed to steal credentials
Did You Know?

Phishing attacks account for over 80% of security incidents! Attackers rely on human error rather than technical vulnerabilities.

How to Recognize Phishing

Learn to spot the warning signs before clicking or responding.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Urgency: "Act now!" or "Your account will be closed!"
  • Suspicious Sender: Email addresses that don't match the organization
  • Poor Grammar: Spelling and grammatical errors
  • Generic Greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Mismatched URLs: Links that go to different destinations
  • Unexpected Attachments: Files you didn't request
  • Too Good To Be True: Offers that seem amazing
Important

When in doubt, don't click! Contact the supposed sender through a known, trusted channel to verify.

Social Engineering Tactics

Social engineering exploits human psychology rather than technical flaws. Attackers manipulate people into breaking security procedures.

Common Social Engineering Techniques

  • Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario to engage the victim
  • Baiting: Offering something enticing to spark curiosity
  • Tailgating: Following authorized personnel into secure areas
  • Quid Pro Quo: Offering a service in exchange for information
  • Intimidation: Using authority or threats to pressure victims
Pro Tip

Always verify requests for sensitive information through official channels, even if the request seems to come from a colleague or boss!

How to Verify Suspicious Messages

  1. Check the Sender: Hover over email addresses to see actual addresses
  2. Inspect Links: Before clicking, hover to see the actual URL destination
  3. Contact Directly: Use official contact info from the company's website
  4. Look for HTTPS: Secure sites have lock icons in the address bar
  5. Check for Personalization: Legitimate companies usually address you by name
  6. Trust Your Gut: If something feels off, it probably is

Prevention Best Practices

  • Think before you click - verify unexpected messages
  • Never provide passwords or sensitive info via email
  • Use email filtering and security software
  • Report phishing attempts to your IT department
  • Keep your software and systems updated
  • Educate yourself about current scam trends
Final Reminder

If you've clicked on a suspicious link or provided information, change your passwords immediately and notify the appropriate parties!
