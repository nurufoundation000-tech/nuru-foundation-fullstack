Objective

🔧 Lab 1: ARP Analysis

The Address Resolution Protocol (ARP) maps IP addresses to MAC addresses inside a local network. ARP traffic can reveal network layout, but also attacks like ARP poisoning.

Filter: arp
Observe: IP to MAC mappings, duplicate or suspicious entries
📡 What to look for
  • Normal ARP requests: “Who has 192.168.1.1?”
  • ARP replies: MAC address announcements
  • Duplicate IP-to-MAC mappings (possible spoofing)
  • Unsolicited ARP replies (gratuitous ARP)
⚠️ Suspicious ARP pattern
  • One IP address associated with multiple MACs → ARP spoofing
  • High frequency of ARP replies from non-gateway devices
Pro tip

In a healthy network, each IP should map to exactly one MAC address (at a given moment). ARP poisoning attacks flood the network with fake mappings.

🔧 Lab 2: DNS Analysis

DNS turns domain names into IP addresses. Attackers often abuse DNS to redirect users or exfiltrate data.

Filter: dns
Observe: Domain requests, returned IP addresses, query types (A, AAAA, TXT)
🌐 Normal DNS traffic
  • Client queries → DNS response with IP address
  • Short TTLs for load balancing
  • Queries for known services (google.com, cloudflare.com)
🚩 Suspicious DNS signs
  • Responses with unexpected IP addresses (DNS spoofing)
  • Long domain names with random characters (DGA / C2)
  • High rate of NXDOMAIN replies (domain not found)
DNS tunneling

Attackers can hide data inside DNS queries. Look for unusually large DNS packets or TXT records carrying encoded payloads.

🔧 Lab 3: TCP Analysis

TCP is the backbone of reliable communication. Understanding its flags and handshake helps detect connection abuse.

Filter: tcp
Observe: SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK sequence, connection patterns, retransmissions
🔌 Healthy TCP conversation
  • SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK (3-way handshake)
  • Data exchange with ACK flags
  • FIN or RST to close connection
⚠️ Anomalies in TCP
  • SYN floods: thousands of SYNs without ACKs
  • RST packets from unexpected sources (session hijacking)
  • Unusual sequence numbers
  • Repeated retransmissions (packet loss or active interception)
Handshake analysis

Use tcp.flags.syn==1 and tcp.flags.ack==0 to isolate initial SYN packets. A sudden spike indicates possible SYN flood attack.

⚠️ Indicators of Suspicious Traffic

Network defenders rely on behavioral anomalies. The following patterns often appear before, during, or after an intrusion.

📈 High number of repeated requests
Same destination, same port → scanning or DDoS
❓ Unknown IP addresses
Outbound connections to geographies or ASNs you don’t recognize
🔁 Repeated failed connections
Brute force attempts (SSH, RDP, SMTP)
📦 Unusual packet sizes
Oversized or fragmented packets, often used for evasion or tunneling
⏱️ Irregular timing
Beaconing traffic (periodic check-ins to C2 servers)
🧬 Protocol misuse
ICMP with payload, DNS on non-standard ports
Real-world note

Modern attackers mix with normal traffic. The key is to spot deviation from baseline — not just “bad” signatures.

🧾 Outcome

After completing the three labs and understanding the suspicious indicators, you will gain two critical cybersecurity abilities:

  • Ability to analyze protocols – Inspect ARP, DNS, and TCP traffic like an analyst. Distinguish between normal handshakes and malicious floods.
  • Ability to identify unusual behavior in traffic – Detect anomalies such as ARP poisoning, DNS tunneling, SYN floods, and irregular packet patterns.
From data to decision

Traffic analysis transforms raw packets into actionable intelligence. You become the person who can answer: “Is something wrong inside our network?”

📝 Assignment

Apply traffic analysis to a real capture (PCAP) or a simulated scenario. Write a short report (1–2 pages) answering:

🔍 Part 1 – ARP analysis
  • Load a sample PCAP (or use Wireshark's sample captures).
  • Apply arp filter. Identify the default gateway's MAC and IP.
  • List any duplicate IP-to-MAC mappings or unsolicited ARP replies.
🌐 Part 2 – DNS & TCP
  • Filter dns and find at least three unique domain queries.
  • Identify a complete TCP handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) and note the timestamp and ports.
  • Mention any TCP retransmissions or RST packets.
Deliverable
  • Write a 300–500 word analysis that includes:
  • ✓ Observations from ARP, DNS and TCP filters
  • ✓ At least one suspicious indicator you identified (or explain why the traffic appears normal)
  • ✓ A conclusion: based on the traffic, would you classify this network as healthy or under attack? Why?

Optional: Use your own home network capture (with permission) or download a malware traffic sample from online repositories.
