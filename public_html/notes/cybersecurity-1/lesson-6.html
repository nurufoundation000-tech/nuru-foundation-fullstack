1. Discovering Open Ports

Ports are like doors into your computer. Some stay open for valid reasons, but every open door is a potential entry point for a hacker.

⚠️ Open Port: Accepting connections. (A potential risk).
✅ Closed Port: Ignoring knocks. (The safe default).
🛡️ Filtered Port: A firewall guard is checking IDs.

LAB: Finding Open Ports on YOUR Device

Use the netstat command to see what is "Listening" for a connection.

Windows: netstat -an | find "LISTENING"
Mac/Linux: netstat -tuln

Understanding Your Netstat Output

When you run the command, you will see a table of connections. Here is how to read it like a professional:

Proto Local Address Foreign Address State
TCP 0.0.0.0:445 0.0.0.0:* LISTENING
  • Local Address: Your IP and the Port number (e.g., :445).
  • Foreign Address: Who is connecting to you (* means anyone).
  • State: LISTENING means the port is open and waiting for data.

2. Advanced Port Scanning

Sometimes you need to see your network from the outside. This is exactly what a hacker does during the reconnaissance phase.

Try an Online Scan: Visit yougetsignal.com to see which ports are visible to the entire internet through your public IP.

⚠️ The Golden Rule of Scanning

NEVER scan a network or an IP address that you do not own or have explicit written permission to test. Unauthorized scanning can be flagged as an attack!

Safe Scanning Target: Use 127.0.0.1 (Localhost) to scan your own machine only.

3. The "Why It Matters" Exercise

Every port serves a purpose, but many are "classic" targets for automated hacker bots.

Port Service The Hacker's Goal
21 FTP Steal files or upload malware.
22 SSH Brute force the password to take full control.
23 Telnet "Sniff" the password because it isn't encrypted.
3389 RDP Remotely log in and view your desktop screen.

4. Securing Your Open Ports

Defense happens in layers. Determine which level you are currently at and how to reach the next one.

Level 1: Basic User

- Keep Firewall ON.
- Turn off unused "Sharing" settings.
- Install all OS updates.

Level 2: Power User

- Set custom Firewall Rules.
- Change default Port numbers.
- Use a VPN for remote access.

Level 3: Professional

- Intrusion Detection (IDS).
- Port Knocking (Hidden ports).
- Regular Vulnerability Scans.

Hands-On: Port Hardening

Let's look at the "Guard" of your computer: The Firewall.

For Windows Users:

  • Open the Run dialog (Win + R) and type wf.msc.
  • Explore Inbound Rules to see every app allowed to "listen" for connections.

For Mac Users:

  • Go to System Preferences -> Security & Privacy -> Firewall.
  • Check Firewall Options to see which applications are allowed to accept incoming data.
Challenge: Identify one app on your list that you no longer use and disable its rule.

🛡️ Your Personal Port Audit Checklist

Immediate Actions

  • Run netstat & identify ports.
  • Verify Firewall is active.
  • Close Bluetooth/File sharing.

Ongoing Habits

  • Monthly port scans of local devices.
  • Review firewall rules every 3 months.
  • Check router firmware for updates.
