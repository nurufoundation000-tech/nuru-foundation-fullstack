Internet Safety & Communication Crew

Welcome to your essential guide for navigating the digital world safely and respectfully. Whether you're a student, teacher, or parent, these skills will help create a safer, kinder internet for everyone.

Internet Safety: Your Digital Shield

What Internet Safety Does: Internet safety acts as your personal protection system against online dangers. It encompasses a range of practices and knowledge that work together to keep you secure whilst browsing, shopping, gaming, or communicating online.

Essential Skills to Master:

  • Creating and managing strong, unique passwords for each account
  • Recognising phishing scams and fraudulent emails
  • Identifying fake websites that try to steal your information
  • Understanding privacy settings on social media platforms
  • Knowing when to ask for help from a trusted adult

Why This Matters: Your personal information is valuable. Internet safety skills prevent identity theft, protect your family's financial information, and ensure your online accounts remain secure. These practices become second nature with regular use.

Digital Communication: Speaking Online with Respect

Clear Messaging: Digital communication teaches you how to express yourself clearly and respectfully in emails, chats, and posts. Tone can be difficult to convey online, so choosing words carefully is essential.

Building Connections: Learn to write polite messages that create positive impressions. Understanding when to use formal versus casual language helps you communicate appropriately in different situations.

Emoji Etiquette: Emojis can enhance messages, but using them wisely is key. Learn which emojis are appropriate for school assignments, which work for friends, and when to avoid them altogether.

Strong digital communication skills help you build and maintain good relationships online, whether with classmates, teachers, or future employers. These skills will serve you throughout your education and career.

Cyber Etiquette: The Golden Rules

Think Before You Type: Would you say it face-to-face? If not, don't type it. Online words are permanent and can spread quickly.

Respect Others' Time and Space: Avoid sending multiple messages in a row. Don't expect instant replies—people have lives beyond screens.

Give Credit Where Due: Always acknowledge others' work, ideas, and creative content. Plagiarism is never acceptable online or offline.

Use Appropriate Language: Avoid ALL CAPS (it feels like shouting), excessive punctuation, or rude comments that hurt others.

Why Cyber Etiquette Transforms the Internet

Creates Positive Spaces: When everyone follows cyber etiquette rules, online communities become welcoming, supportive places where people can learn, share, and connect without fear of harassment or negativity.

Builds Your Reputation: Your online behaviour creates a digital footprint. Universities and employers often review social media profiles. Demonstrating good cyber etiquette shows maturity and professionalism.

Remember: Behind every screen is a real person with feelings. Treat others as you wish to be treated, and help make the internet a better place for everyone.

Recognising Threats: Spotting Danger Early

Suspicious Links: Hover over links before clicking to see the real destination. Be wary of shortened URLs or links in unexpected messages, even from people you know.

Strange Messages: Watch for spelling errors, urgent demands, or requests for personal information. Legitimate organisations rarely ask for passwords or bank details via email.

Too Good to Be True Offers: Free prizes, incredible discounts, or shocking news designed to make you click quickly are usually scams. Stop and think before acting.

Fake Websites: Check for secure connections (https://), look for trust badges, and verify web addresses carefully. Scammers create fake sites that look nearly identical to real ones.

Examples of Dangerous Links to Avoid

Urgent Security Alerts: `yourbank-security-alert.com/verify` - Real banks never send links asking you to verify account details. Always navigate to your bank's website directly by typing the address yourself.

Prize Winner Notifications: `claim-your-prize-now.net/winner123` - If you didn't enter a competition, you haven't won. These links often install malware or steal personal information.

Suspicious Shortened URLs: `bit.ly/xy34zq` or `tinyurl.com/abc123` - Shortened links hide the real destination. Only click these from trusted sources, and use URL expander tools to check where they lead first.

Fake Social Media Messages: `faceb00k-security.com/login` - Notice the zeros instead of 'o's? Scammers use similar-looking addresses. Always check spelling carefully.

Phishing Email Links: `paypaI-resolve.com/dispute` - That's a capital 'i' instead of 'l' in PayPal. These tiny changes are easy to miss but signal danger.

Fake Delivery Notifications: `royalmail-redelivery.co/track` - Real delivery companies use their official domains. This fake link might steal credit card details or install viruses.

Unexpected File Sharing: `dropb0x-share.net/document.exe` - Be suspicious of unexpected file shares, especially executable files (.exe). These often contain malware.

Golden Rule: When in doubt, don't click! Navigate to websites directly by typing addresses yourself, or ask a trusted adult for help.

Why Threat Recognition Prevents Disasters

Stops Identity Theft: Recognising threats before clicking prevents criminals from accessing your personal information, photos, and accounts. Identity theft can take years to resolve and causes serious problems for families.

Protects Your Devices: Avoiding malicious links keeps viruses, ransomware, and spyware off your computer, tablet, or phone. These threats can delete important files or spy on everything you do online.

Safeguards Others: When your accounts stay secure, hackers can't use them to send scams to your friends and family. Your vigilance protects your entire network of contacts from potential harm.

Your Action Plan: Staying Safe Online

Review Your Knowledge Daily: Make internet safety a habit, not an afterthought. Before clicking any link, take three seconds to assess whether it looks legitimate.

Communicate Openly: Talk with parents, teachers, and friends about online experiences. Share suspicious messages you receive and discuss why they're dangerous.

Practice Cyber Etiquette: Be the change you wish to see online. Treat others with kindness, give credit for others' work, and stand up against cyberbullying.

Keep Learning: Threats evolve constantly. Stay informed about new scams, update your passwords regularly, and never stop improving your digital literacy skills.

Together, we can create a safer, more respectful internet for everyone. Your commitment to these principles makes a real difference in the digital world.
