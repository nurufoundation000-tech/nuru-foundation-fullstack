Machine Learning Basics

Welcome to Module 2, where we explore the fascinating world of how computers learn from data. Machine learning isn't magic—it's a systematic approach to teaching computers to recognise patterns, make decisions, and improve their performance over time.

How Computers Learn: Three Fundamental Approaches

At the heart of machine learning lie three distinct learning paradigms, each suited to different types of problems. Understanding these approaches is essential for any aspiring machine learning practitioner, as they form the foundation upon which more complex systems are built.

  • Supervised Learning: The computer learns from labelled examples, like a student learning with an answer key. You provide both the questions and the correct answers, allowing the algorithm to identify patterns and relationships.
  • Unsupervised Learning: The computer discovers hidden patterns in unlabelled data without being told what to look for. It's like exploring a new city without a map—you find your own routes and landmarks.
  • Reinforcement Learning: The computer learns through trial and error, receiving rewards for good actions and penalties for mistakes. Think of it as training a pet with treats and encouragement.

Supervised Learning: Learning with a Teacher

Supervised learning is perhaps the most intuitive form of machine learning. In this approach, we provide the computer with a training dataset containing both input features and their corresponding correct outputs (labels). The algorithm studies these examples to learn the underlying relationship between inputs and outputs, enabling it to make predictions on new, unseen data.

How it works:

Imagine teaching a child to identify animals. You show them photographs and tell them "This is a cat" or "This is a dog." After seeing enough examples, the child learns to recognise the distinguishing features—pointy ears, whiskers, tail shape—and can identify new animals they've never seen before.

Real-world applications:

  • Email spam detection
  • Medical diagnosis from symptoms
  • House price prediction
  • Credit risk assessment
  • Image classification systems

The key advantage of supervised learning is its accuracy when sufficient labelled data is available. However, creating these labelled datasets can be time-consuming and expensive, particularly for complex problems requiring expert knowledge.

Classic Example:

Show the computer 100 pictures of cats 🐱 and dogs 🐶, clearly labelling which is which. The algorithm learns to distinguish between them based on features like ear shape, nose structure, and body proportions.

Unsupervised Learning: Finding Hidden Patterns

Unsupervised learning represents a more exploratory approach to machine learning. Here, the computer receives data without any labels or predetermined categories. Its task is to discover structure, patterns, and relationships within the data entirely on its own. This mirrors how humans naturally notice patterns in the world around us without being explicitly taught.

The clustering challenge:

One of the most common unsupervised learning tasks is clustering, where the algorithm groups similar data points together. For instance, an e-commerce platform might use unsupervised learning to segment customers based on their browsing and purchasing behaviour, discovering groups like "budget-conscious shoppers," "luxury buyers," or "impulse purchasers"—all without being told these categories exist.

Applications:

  • Customer Segmentation: Grouping customers by shopping habits, preferences, and behaviours to enable targeted marketing campaigns.
  • Genetic Research: Identifying gene expression patterns that might indicate disease susceptibility or treatment responses.
  • Topic Modelling: Automatically discovering themes and subjects within large collections of text documents.

The beauty of unsupervised learning lies in its ability to reveal insights we might never have thought to look for. However, interpreting these patterns requires human expertise and domain knowledge to ensure the discoveries are meaningful and actionable.

Reinforcement Learning: Learning Through Experience

Reinforcement learning takes inspiration from how humans and animals learn through interaction with their environment. Rather than learning from a fixed dataset, a reinforcement learning agent learns by taking actions, observing the consequences, and adjusting its behaviour to maximize rewards over time.

The reward mechanism:

Consider a robot learning to walk. Initially, it stumbles randomly, but each time it takes a step forward without falling, it receives a positive reward. When it falls, it receives a negative reward (or penalty). Through thousands of attempts, the robot gradually learns which movements lead to successful walking.

  1. Observe State: The agent perceives its current situation in the environment.
  2. Take Action: Based on its observations, the agent chooses and executes an action.
  3. Receive Reward: The environment provides feedback in the form of rewards or penalties.
  4. Update Policy: The agent adjusts its decision-making strategy to improve future performance.

Reinforcement learning has achieved remarkable success in game-playing AI (like AlphaGo), autonomous vehicles, robotics, and resource optimization problems. Its trial-and-error nature makes it particularly suited to scenarios where the optimal strategy isn't known in advance and must be discovered through exploration.

Project Deep Dive: Handwritten Digit Recognition

The MNIST Dataset

The Modified National Institute of Standards and Technology (MNIST) database contains 70,000 images of handwritten digits (0-9), each 28×28 pixels in grayscale. It's the perfect starting point because the images are small, clean, and the dataset is well-documented with extensive community support.

Project workflow:

  1. Data loading: Import the MNIST dataset using libraries like TensorFlow or PyTorch
  2. Exploration: Visualise sample images to understand your data
  3. Preprocessing: Normalise pixel values (typically scaling to 0-1 range)
  4. Model building: Create a simple neural network with input, hidden, and output layers
  5. Training: Feed the network training examples and adjust weights
  6. Evaluation: Test your model on unseen data to measure accuracy

Expected Results

Even a basic neural network can achieve 95-97% accuracy on MNIST. More sophisticated architectures like convolutional neural networks (CNNs) can reach 99%+ accuracy!

Common challenges: Beginners often struggle with overfitting (model memorises training data rather than learning patterns) and underfitting (model is too simple to capture patterns). Learning to balance model complexity is a crucial skill you'll develop through this project.

Project Deep Dive: Spam Email Classification

Email spam filtering demonstrates how machine learning handles messy, real-world text data. Unlike the clean images in MNIST, email text is highly variable, containing slang, typos, multiple languages, and deliberately obfuscated content designed to evade filters.

Data Collection

Obtain a labelled spam dataset (like the SpamAssassin public corpus or the SMS Spam Collection). These datasets contain thousands of examples of both spam and legitimate messages.

Text Preprocessing

Clean the text by converting to lowercase, removing punctuation, eliminating stop words (common words like "the," "and"), and applying stemming or lemmatisation to reduce words to their root forms.

Feature Extraction

Transform text into numerical features using techniques like Bag of Words or TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency), which capture word importance.

Model Training

Train a classifier (Naive Bayes is excellent for beginners) to distinguish spam from legitimate messages based on word patterns and frequencies.

Evaluation & Iteration

Assess performance using metrics appropriate for imbalanced datasets, then refine your approach based on misclassified examples.

Naive Bayes classifier: This algorithm is particularly effective for spam detection because it calculates the probability that an email is spam based on the words it contains. It assumes features are independent (hence "naive"), which works surprisingly well for text classification despite being a simplification.

Evaluation metrics matter: Accuracy alone can be misleading. If 95% of emails are legitimate, a model that labels everything as "not spam" achieves 95% accuracy but is useless! Focus on precision (what percentage of spam predictions are correct) and recall (what percentage of actual spam is caught).

Your AI Assistant: Using Prompts Effectively

As a beginner, you're not alone in your learning journey. Large language models and AI assistants can provide tremendous support—if you know how to communicate with them effectively. Crafting good prompts is a skill that will accelerate your learning and help you overcome obstacles.

Be Specific About Your Level

Always indicate you're a beginner and specify which tools you're using. Instead of "How do I build a neural network?" try "I'm a beginner using Python and TensorFlow. Can you explain how to build a simple neural network for the MNIST dataset with code comments explaining each step?"

Request Structured Learning

Ask for step-by-step guidance rather than complete solutions. Good prompt: "Give me a simple dataset to practice supervised learning with Python, and outline the 5 main steps I should follow to build my first model."

Ask for Explanations

Don't just copy code—understand it. Include requests like "Please explain why we're using this approach" or "What would happen if we changed this parameter?" in your prompts.

Iterate Based on Results

Share your errors and ask for debugging help: "I'm getting this error message [paste error]. I'm trying to [describe goal]. What might be causing this and how can I fix it?"

Example prompt for getting started: "I'm a complete beginner to machine learning with basic Python knowledge. I want to practice supervised learning. Can you recommend a simple, free dataset that's well-documented for beginners, explain what makes it good for learning, and outline the first three steps I should take to build a basic model? Please include which Python libraries I'll need to install."
